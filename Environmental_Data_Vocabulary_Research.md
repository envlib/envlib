# **Controlled Vocabularies and Semantic Interoperability in Environmental and Geospatial Data Models**

The integration of environmental and geospatial data across disparate scientific domains demands robust mechanisms for achieving syntactic and semantic interoperability. As observational data volumes scale exponentially—driven by high-frequency sensor networks, earth observation satellites, expansive citizen science initiatives, and automated digital twin infrastructures—the primary challenge in environmental informatics has shifted from data collection to data harmonization. Controlled vocabularies, ontologies, and standardized data models form the essential cyberinfrastructure required to render these massive datasets Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, and Reusable (FAIR). By formalizing the definitions of measured phenomena, geographic features, and sampling methodologies, these semantic tools prevent data fragmentation and enable the automated reasoning required for global-scale environmental modeling.  
The landscape of environmental informatics is supported by several foundational frameworks, including the Open Geospatial Consortium’s (OGC) Observations and Measurements (O\&M) standard, the joint W3C/OGC Semantic Sensor Network (SOSA/SSN) ontology, and the Observations Data Model 2 (ODM2). These structural models act as the scaffolding, which is subsequently populated by highly curated controlled vocabularies and ontologies such as the Climate and Forecast (CF) conventions, the Environment Ontology (ENVO), and the Semantic Web for Earth and Environmental Terminology (SWEET). The resolution of Uniform Resource Identifiers (URIs) and the active governance of these concepts are handled by sophisticated, machine-actionable registries, including the NERC Vocabulary Server (NVS), the Marine Metadata Interoperability Ontology Registry and Repository (MMISW ORR), and the European Bioinformatics Institute Ontology Lookup Service (EBI OLS). Furthermore, crowd-sourced platforms and generalized property graphs, such as OpenStreetMap (OSM) and Wikidata, introduce alternative, massive-scale methodologies for cataloging the physical world, presenting unique challenges and opportunities for formal semantic alignment.  
This report provides an exhaustive analysis of these data models, their associated vocabularies, the registries that host them, and the complex interoperability crosswalks required to bridge the gap between feature-based geographic information systems (GIS) and observation-based sensor networks.

## **Foundational Environmental Data Models**

The foundation of geospatial and environmental interoperability lies in the structural data models that dictate how observations, features, and sensors are digitally represented and linked. The transition from legacy relational databases to linked-data architectures has necessitated the development of highly modular, feature-based frameworks capable of expressing complex spatiotemporal relationships.

### **Observations, Measurements, and Samples (O\&M) and SOSA/SSN**

The OGC Observations and Measurements (O\&M) standard (also formalized as ISO 19156\) provides a core conceptual schema for encoding observations and the features involved in sampling.1 Originally developed within the context of geographic information systems based on general patterns proposed by Fowler and Odell, O\&M is not strictly limited to geospatial data, allowing it to serve as the response model for the broader Sensor Web Enablement (SWE) suite, including the Sensor Observation Service (SOS).2 The schema establishes critical relationships between sampling artefacts and real-world features of interest, defining core properties such as the sampled feature, the related observation, and complexes of associated sampling features.2  
To bridge the O\&M standard with modern Web of Things and Linked Data paradigms, the joint W3C and OGC Spatial Data on the Web Working Group developed the Semantic Sensor Network (SSN) ontology and its lightweight core module, SOSA (Sensor, Observation, Sample, and Actuator).3 SOSA and SSN provide a flexible yet coherent perspective for representing the entities, relations, and activities involved in sensing and sampling, utilizing minimal axiomatization to support a broad spectrum of use cases, ranging from satellite imagery and large-scale scientific monitoring to industrial infrastructure and citizen science.5  
The integration of geospatial features and environmental observations within SOSA/SSN is achieved through a distinct separation of concerns, resolving fundamental ambiguities in legacy systems 4:

* **sosa:FeatureOfInterest**: Represents the real-world geospatial or environmental entity being studied, such as a river catchment, an atmospheric layer, or a specific geographic region.4  
* **sosa:ObservableProperty**: Defines the specific characteristic under observation, such as flow velocity, atmospheric pressure, or ambient temperature.4  
* **sosa:Observation**: Acts as the central event node linking the sensor, the feature, the property, and the resulting quantitative or qualitative data (the result time and the phenomenon time).4  
* **sosa:Sample and sosa:Sampling**: These classes address the practical reality that environmental features are rarely observed in their absolute entirety. Instead, representative sub-units (e.g., ice cores, water aliquots, soil profiles) are extracted via defined procedures.4 The sample serves as a proxy for the ultimate feature of interest.  
* **sosa:Platform**: An entity, such as a satellite, weather station, or marine buoy, that hosts sensors and provides the critical spatial context and trajectory for where observations occur.4

This architectural distinction is critical for provenance. By treating observations and sampling as dynamic activities (aligned horizontally with the prov:Activity class in the PROV-O ontology) and features and samples as static objects (prov:Entity), SOSA/SSN ensures precise spatiotemporal tracking.4 However, because SOSA operates under the Open World Assumption inherent to the Semantic Web—meaning that omitted data is not assumed to be false—enforcing strict structural compliance requires secondary validation mechanisms. The Shapes Constraint Language (SHACL) is frequently deployed to validate SOSA/SSN observation graphs.7 SHACL applies a Closed World Assumption to specific data shapes, ensuring that datasets adhere to the expected structural constraints (e.g., verifying that an observation possesses exactly one result time and one linked feature of interest) before integration into broader knowledge graphs.8

### **Observations Data Model 2 (ODM2)**

While SOSA/SSN provides a high-level RDF ontology for the Semantic Web, the Observations Data Model 2 (ODM2) offers a concrete, operational information model and software ecosystem optimized for feature-based Earth observations.10 ODM2 acts as an implementation profile of the OGC O\&M standard, designed explicitly to facilitate interoperability across disparate scientific domains and distributed cyberinfrastructures, operating effectively within Service Oriented Architectures (SOA).10  
Developed through extensive community input to integrate and enhance concepts from the CUAHSI Hydrological Information System (ODM1), IEDA PetDB, and SedDB, ODM2 handles a vast array of complex datasets.10 Its schema accommodates hydrological time series, soil and sediment geochemistry, biodiversity surveys, oceanographic sensor profiles, and multi-dimensional spectra.10 The architecture of ODM2 revolves around several core extensions that deeply embed controlled vocabularies into the data lifecycle:

* **Sampling Features Extension:** This component tracks complex, nested hierarchies of spatial and physical entities. It manages sampling sites (weather stations, stream gauges, boreholes), field specimens (rocks, cores, plants, water), and laboratory specimens (digests, extracts, powders).10 This extension maintains full conceptual compatibility with the International Geo Sample Numbers (IGSN).10  
* **Action Tracking:** ODM2 records discrete "Actions" (such as sensor deployments, specimen collections, or laboratory analyses), the relationships between these sequential actions, the personnel executing them, the specific equipment deployed, and the resulting analytical values.10

To enforce semantic and syntactic consistency across diverse implementations, ODM2 relies on a native ecosystem of controlled vocabularies managed via a Python/Django-based web application and community moderation system.11 These vocabularies define specific entity relationships and typing mechanisms 12:

| ODM2 Vocabulary Domain | Function within the Information Model Architecture |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Action Type** | Classifies the nature of the operations performed (e.g., field sampling, instrument calibration, deployment), dictating whether an observation result is logically expected from the action.12 |
| **Result Type** | Distinguishes the structure of the resulting data. ODM2 deliberately separates "Results" from the underlying data "Values" to allow for varied physical storage structures (e.g., time series arrays vs. singular laboratory measurements).12 |
| **Relationship Type** | Defines the semantics of physical and logical connections between entities. For example, specifying that a laboratory specimen was derived from a field specimen that was collected at a specific geographic site.12 |
| **Property Data Type** | Governs the primitive programmatic data types required for extension properties that are dynamically appended to Sites, Variables, or Results.12 |
| **Aggregation Statistic** | Describes the calculated statistic associated with recorded observations over a specific time aggregation interval (e.g., daily mean, hourly maximum).12 |

ODM2 instances can be accessed, queried, and manipulated using the ODM2 Python API, which utilizes Object-Relational-Mapping (SQLAlchemy) to translate entity types into a programmable class-subclass structure, enabling the inclusion of domain-specific attributes.10  
Data exchange within the ODM2 ecosystem is further standardized through the YAML Observation Data Archive & Exchange (YODA) format.10 YODA files are self-describing, machine-parsable, text-based documents that encapsulate both quantitative data and relational metadata within a single package.14 A YODA file is structurally validated against required and optional ODM2 fields and controlled vocabularies using JSON Schema via utilities hosted in the YODA-Tools repository.14 This methodology has been adopted by the Critical Zone Observatory (CZO) archive to guide investigators using Excel data entry templates that automatically generate compliant YODA files, ensuring that all archived sensor and sample data adhere strictly to the ODM2 vocabularies.15

## **Primary Environmental Ontologies and Vocabularies**

The data models described above—O\&M, SOSA/SSN, and ODM2—require authoritative, conceptually rigorous vocabularies to populate their attributes and describe the physical phenomena being observed. The Environment Ontology (ENVO) and the Climate and Forecast (CF) conventions represent two dominant, yet structurally divergent, approaches to this requirement.

### **The Environment Ontology (ENVO)**

The Environment Ontology (ENVO) is a highly expressive, community-driven, Open Biological and Biomedical Ontologies (OBO) Foundry domain ontology designed for the concise, controlled description of environments, ecosystems, habitats, and environmental processes at all scales, from microscopic to intergalactic.17 Aligning tightly with the Basic Formal Ontology (BFO) 2.0—the upper-level ontology of the OBO Foundry—ENVO ensures semantic homogeneity and cross-domain interoperability with other biological and biomedical ontologies, such as the Gene Ontology (GO), the Phenotype And Trait Ontology (PATO), and the Uber-anatomy Ontology (UBERON).17  
ENVO structures environmental knowledge into primary top-level classes to accommodate diverse ecological, genomic, and geospatial applications 17:

1. **Environmental System (ENVO\_01000254):** Defined structurally as a system with the disposition to "environ"—to contain and causally integrate material entities within a specific geographic site.17 This class heavily features biomes (community-centric systems with evolutionary adaptations, such as the tundra biome) and habitats (population-centric systems providing survival components).17  
2. **Environmental Feature (ENVO\_00002297):** Material entities that act as causal hubs or physical supports for environmental systems. Examples include massive geographic, hydrographic, and physiographic features like seamounts, valleys, or shorelines.17  
3. **Environmental Material (ENVO\_00010483):** Masses, volumes, or portions of a medium (e.g., soil, water, permafrost). ENVO recognizes the ontological complexity of these materials, treating them not merely as homogeneous chemical substances but as complex mixtures often containing microbial communities, gas inclusions, and mineral particulates.17  
4. **Environmental Condition (ENVO\_01000203):** Specific ranges of qualities, such as temperature extremes or solar irradiation ranges, that define an environmental state or operational condition.17

ENVO is inherently designed for cross-domain integration, avoiding the creation of knowledge silos. Its stable release versions automatically include imported classes from externally referenced ontologies to ensure broad semantic consistency.17 The ontology is managed using tools like the ELK reasoner for logical consistency checks and the OBO Ontology Release Tool (OORT) for OBO-to-OWL conversions.17  
Furthermore, ENVO utilizes subsets (annotated via the in\_subset property) to provide specialized, trimmed-down slices of the ontology for specific research domains.17 For instance, the envoPolar subset contains terms like alpine glacier and permafrost, while the envoMarine subset contains terms like Bathymodiolus-dominated oceanic mussel reef.17 This modularity allows applications, such as the Genomic Standards Consortium (GSC) or the Encyclopaedia of Life (EOL), to adopt specific environmental descriptors without absorbing the entire computational weight of the full ontology.17

### **The Climate and Forecast (CF) Conventions**

In contrast to ENVO's highly structured, multi-class post-coordinated ontological hierarchy, the Climate and Forecast (CF) conventions provide a flatter, pre-coordinated string-based vocabulary. CF was primarily designed to promote the processing and sharing of files created with the NetCDF API.18 Initially developed for gridded output from climate and oceanographic numerical models, CF conventions have aggressively expanded to encompass broader geosciences and in-situ observational data.18  
A critical component of the CF framework is the CF Standard Name Table, which assigns unique, standardized strings to physical quantities to explicitly identify comparability across disparate data sources.18 The construction of a CF standard name follows extremely strict grammatical guidelines: names consist solely of lowercase letters, digits, and underscores, beginning with a letter, and contain no blank spaces.19  
The syntax concatenates multiple physical realities into a single identifier using a standardized phrase structure. The general form is: \[surface\]\[component\] standard\_name \[at surface\]\[in medium\]\[due to process\]\[assuming condition\].19  
This composite structure creates significant semantic density within a single text string. For example, a single CF standard name encapsulates the property (e.g., temperature), the medium (e.g., sea water), and the spatial constraints (e.g., at the surface). In addition to the name itself, the conventions strictly enforce unit compliance; a variable carrying a standard\_name attribute must utilize units that are physically equivalent to the canonical SI units specified in the CF table.21 This physical equivalency is programmatically verified through UNIDATA's UDUNITS software package (e.g., Celsius is acceptable for a canonical unit of Kelvin because UDUNITS can perform the algorithmic conversion).21  
The CF metadata model also excels in describing complex spatial and temporal configurations intrinsic to geospatial modeling. Variables are annotated with grid\_mapping attributes that link to empty container variables detailing precise coordinate reference systems and map projections (e.g., Lambert\_Conformal).23 Furthermore, the conventions handle temporal and spatial anomalies, generalized mathematically as the difference between a particular measurement value and a baseline norm calculated over specified statistical ranges.23

## **Semantic Registries and Vocabulary Servers**

To ensure that vocabularies like ENVO, CF, and the parameters of ODM2 are machine-actionable, version-controlled, and accessible globally, the scientific community relies on robust semantic registries. These infrastructure nodes apply FAIR principles to the terminologies themselves, providing stable, dereferenceable URIs, rigorous version control, and content negotiation formats (e.g., RDF/XML, Turtle, JSON-LD) required by automated systems.28

### **The NERC Vocabulary Server (NVS)**

The NERC Vocabulary Server (NVS), developed and managed by the British Oceanographic Data Centre (BODC), is a premier semantic repository serving standardized, hierarchically organized terminologies for the marine, atmospheric, and broader environmental domains.30 Operating on the W3C Simple Knowledge Organization System (SKOS) standard, the NVS exposes concepts—defined as discrete units of thought—through both hierarchical schemes (thesauri for discovery) and collections (curated lists for publication and addressing).33  
The NVS resolves the pervasive problem of semantic ambiguity by providing compact, permanent identifiers (URIs) designed for computer storage, alongside human-readable terms, abbreviations, and exhaustive definitions.34 The server utilizes a truly RESTful architecture alongside SPARQL endpoints and legacy SOAP APIs to allow developers to build dynamic drop-down lists, implement verifiable metadata mark-up, and perform automated cross-walks between differing schemata.31  
A critical function of the NVS is its role as the de facto Linked Data host for the CF Standard Names via the highly utilized P07 collection.20 While the official CF standard name table is maintained as an XML document by the CF community, the NVS simultaneously generates SKOS representations of these terms upon every update.35 This translation converts the flat CF strings into a networked knowledge graph, allowing for the mapping of skos:broader and skos:narrower relations, and facilitating the connection of CF names to other ontologies in a machine-actionable format.20 The migration toward recognizing the NVS P07 collection as the canonical, FAIR-compliant source for CF standard names is an ongoing discussion within the CF governance community, driven by the need for advanced functionality like SPARQL querying and I-ADOPT ontology conformance.35

### **MMISW Ontology Registry and Repository (ORR)**

The Marine Metadata Interoperability (MMI) Ontology Registry and Repository (ORR) provides a collaborative, web-based platform tailored for environmental data providers who may lack extensive semantic web engineering experience.37 It allows domain experts to create, host, annotate, and share RDF-based vocabularies.37  
The ORR system automatically generates web-resolvable Internationalized Resource Identifiers (IRIs) for every submitted term.37 It functions in a dual capacity: as a *repository* hosting native ontologies, and as a *registry* cataloging pointers and metadata for externally hosted ontologies.40 The software architecture underlying the MMI ORR is highly scalable and has been deployed to power the Earth Science Information Partners (ESIP) Community Ontology Repository (COR), cementing its role in the broader geosciences community for managing term mappings, semantic inferences, and vocabulary versioning.37

### **EBI Ontology Lookup Service (OLS)**

Developed by the Samples, Phenotypes and Ontologies Team (SPOT) at the European Molecular Biology Laboratory’s European Bioinformatics Institute (EMBL-EBI), the Ontology Lookup Service (OLS) acts as a centralized access point for biomedical and biological ontologies.42  
Given the deep intersection between environmental conditions and biological phenotypes (e.g., microbial ecology, biodiversity surveying), the OLS is a critical repository for environmental informatics. It hosts comprehensive versions of ENVO and related hybrid ontologies such as the Biological and Environmental Research Ontology (BERO)—an application ontology combining subsets from ChEBI, ENVO, the Gene Ontology (GO), and the NCBI Taxonomy.44 The OLS provides interactive visualizations of ontology embeddings, where the semantic similarity of terms is mapped in a two-dimensional space based on Large Language Model (LLM) embeddings of labels, synonyms, and definitions.42 Companion tools like OxO provide explicit cross-ontology mappings, while ZOOMA assists researchers in automatically mapping raw, unstructured textual data to the formal ontologies housed within the OLS framework.42

### **OGC Rainbow and Definitions Server**

The OGC RAINBOW ecosystem, comprising the OGC Definitions Server, provides the authoritative namespace for terms, coordinate reference systems, and concepts defined within OGC standards.28 It applies FAIR principles to the core interoperability targets of spatial information, using stable URIs to unambiguously identify concepts.28 This includes the foundational definitions underpinning the SOSA/SSN standard, GeoSPARQL, the OGC API suite, and the Observations, Measurements and Samples (OMS) conceptual models.1 The OGC Profiling Toolkit supports the continuous integration and deployment of these semantic assets, ensuring that implementation fields (such as spatial reference systems, data formats, or license rights) can be populated via URI links directly referencing the OGC Rainbow repository.48

| Registry Platform | Managing Body | Core Technical Framework | Primary Domain Focus |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **NVS** | BODC / NERC | SKOS, REST, SPARQL | Oceanography, Atmospheric Sciences, Geosciences 31 |
| **MMISW ORR** | MMI / ESIP | RDF, OWL, IRI resolution | Marine Metadata, General Earth Sciences 37 |
| **EBI OLS** | EMBL-EBI SPOT | OWL, API, LLM Embeddings | Biomedical, Biological, Ecological (ENVO) 42 |
| **OGC Rainbow** | Open Geospatial Consortium | Linked Data, SKOS | Geospatial Standards, Coordinate Systems, Sensor Networks 28 |

## **Alternative Taxonomies: Folksonomies and Graph Databases**

While expert-driven, top-down ontologies like ENVO and SOSA ensure high scientific rigor, crowdsourced and collaborative models provide alternative mechanisms for mapping the environment at a massive, global scale. These platforms introduce significant data volume but require complex reconciliation to achieve formal semantic interoperability.

### **OpenStreetMap (OSM) Folksonomy**

OpenStreetMap relies on an unstructured, free-tagging folksonomy to represent physical features on the ground.49 Geographic attributes are attached to spatial primitives (nodes, ways, and relations) as simple key-value pairs (e.g., highway=footway, amenity=college, natural=water).49  
This bottom-up approach allows the mapping community to describe an unlimited number of attributes and rapidly adapt to newly unmapped phenomenon without waiting for formal committee approval or schema updates.49 However, the thematic dimension of OSM data is exceptionally difficult to process computationally due to extreme semantic heterogeneity, alias usage, and varying granularity.52 Navigating the mapping of these folksonomies to formal ontologies is an ongoing research challenge. Tools like ShapeChange have been utilized in OGC Testbeds (such as Testbed-12) to convert UML models of geographic information into OWL ontologies in accordance with ISO 19150-2.53 This attempts to bridge the gap between unstructured geographic primitives in OSM and formal semantic representations required by rigorous environmental models. Recently, generative AI tools (such as ChatGPT) have been explored to assist contributors in identifying complex, unstandardized tag combinations for highly specific environmental or commercial features, further illustrating the fluidity of the OSM folksonomy.56

### **Wikidata Property Graphs**

Wikidata operates as a massive, open community generalized property graph that tightly integrates with Wikipedia.57 Utilizing the Wikibase data model, it provides a metamodel for describing real-world entities through structured statements consisting of Items (Q-nodes), Properties (P-nodes), and Values.59  
For environmental and geospatial observations, Wikidata acts as an immensely powerful intermediary linking hub. It supports highly specific properties that parallel concepts found in SOSA and ENVO. For example, Wikidata utilizes Property P111 to define the "measured physical quantity" (conceptually equivalent to sosa:ObservableProperty), and properties like P1057 (chromosome), P644 (genomic start), and P645 (genomic end) to spatially encode biological data.57  
Furthermore, Wikidata acts as a critical cross-reference engine. Property P7471 links geographic elements directly to their corresponding "iNaturalist place ID," bridging open-access demographic and spatial boundary data with citizen-science biodiversity observation networks.61 Advanced geospatial knowledge graphs, such as KnowWhereGraph, explicitly extend this logic.62 KnowWhereGraph integrates over 29 billion RDF triples representing environmental and human observations, directly utilizing the SOSA/SSN ontology alongside the OWL Time Ontology and QUDT (Quantities, Units, Dimensions and Data Types).62 To manage this scale, it employs a library of generic SOSA-SHACL shape constraints to verify that the materialized graph complies with SOSA recommendations, reporting any structural violations.9

## **Semantic Interoperability: Crosswalks, Overlaps, and Implementation Challenges**

The existence of multiple robust standards (e.g., CF, ENVO, SOSA, Wikidata) necessitates the development of semantic crosswalks to facilitate data translation. However, mapping these ontologies is rarely a trivial one-to-one exercise due to fundamental differences in architectural philosophy, granularity, and the treatment of dimensions.

### **Mapping CF Conventions to ENVO and SWEET**

Mappings between CF standard names, the SWEET ontology, and the Global Change Master Directory (GCMD) science keywords have been generated historically to assist data discovery across domains.20 However, the maintenance of these crosswalks is resource-intensive and often falls out of synchronization with the rapidly evolving CF baseline standard.20  
The fundamental challenge in establishing exact matches (e.g., utilizing skos:exactMatch) between CF standard names and ENVO classes lies in their structural divergence. ENVO provides a multidimensional, post-coordinated hierarchy where entities are strictly segregated ontologically; "sea water" is a material entity (ENVO\_00002149), while "temperature" is a quality or condition.17 Conversely, the CF convention concatenates these distinct concepts into a single, pre-coordinated text string (sea\_water\_temperature).19  
To automatically map these concepts, semantic engines must deconstruct the CF string into its constituent parts (medium, property, process, location) and establish complex relational graphs linking them to individual ENVO or SWEET nodes.64 Automated ontology matching tools, which rely on pairwise matching schemas or Hausdorff distance estimation, struggle with the linguistic and dimensional parsing required to guarantee accuracy across such disparate data models.64 Consequently, manual intervention is frequently required to curate these alignments.64 The NERC Vocabulary Server alleviates this partially by representing CF terms as SKOS concepts, enabling the definition of hierarchical skos:broader and skos:narrower relationships within the CF vocabulary itself, thus creating a more navigable semantic network for cross-referencing.20

### **Bridging Feature-Based and Observation-Based Models (ELFIE/SELFIE)**

A broader interoperability challenge exists between traditional Geographic Information Systems (GIS), which are inherently feature-based, and sensor networks, which are observation-based.68 Traditional GIS architectures model the world as static polygons, lines, and points representing physical objects annotated with thematic attributes. Sensor networks, utilizing SOSA/SSN or O\&M, model the world as a series of temporal events (observations) that yield quantitative values regarding a specific property of a feature.4 Resolving this impedance mismatch is crucial for the development of modern Digital Twins, Analysis Ready Data (ARD) cubes, and smart infrastructure.68  
A primary technological solution investigated by the OGC is the integration of JSON-LD (JavaScript Object Notation for Linked Data) within standard geospatial APIs. The OGC Environmental Linked Features Interoperability Experiment (ELFIE) and its successor (SELFIE) directly addressed the methodology for exposing links between environmental domain features (e.g., a river catchment polygon) and sampling features (e.g., a water quality monitoring station).74  
ELFIE utilized JSON-LD documents to encapsulate both topological models and related observational data.74 By injecting JSON-LD @id keys into API payloads, systems can assign fully dereferenceable HTTP URIs to non-information resources (the physical environment itself) and the data artifacts representing them.76 The experiments demonstrated that OGC API \- Features can successfully serve as a foundational mechanism for retrieving these linked data graphs.75 Implementations in major server software, such as the JSON-LD plugins for PyGeoAPI and GeoServer, provide practical pathways for deployment.77 This approach ensures that when a client queries a geospatial feature, the payload contains semantic links resolving directly to observational datasets described by ODM2 or O\&M, harmonized via shared ENVO or CF vocabularies.75

## **Regional and Domain-Specific Ecosystems: Case Studies**

The translation of these theoretical models into operational infrastructure is best observed through national and institutional deployments. Organizations in New Zealand and Australia have been particularly proactive in applying Linked Data, SKOS, and standardized data models to environmental management and reporting.

### **New Zealand: Manaaki Whenua, LINZ, and NIWA**

In New Zealand, environmental, land-resource, and atmospheric data are heavily curated through specific informatics initiatives aligning with international standards to support the Government Enterprise Architecture (GEA-NZ).79  
Manaaki Whenua – Landcare Research (MWLR) manages the Land Resource Information System (LRIS), which houses authoritative datasets including the New Zealand Land Resource Inventory (NZLRI) and S-map.81 The NZLRI maps land management units based on five critical physical factors (rock type, soil, slope, erosion, and vegetation) and assigns Land Use Capability (LUC) classes indicating sustainable production capacity.81 To standardize the publication of this data, the Innovative Data Analysis (IDA) programme developed the IDA Linked Data Registry.88 Operating on Semantic Web standards, this registry functions as a lightweight ontology to formalize New Zealand's land-use classes, soil vocabularies, and property definitions.88 The infrastructure utilizes data cube technologies (such as Open Data Cube) to store multi-dimensional arrays of soil and land data, allowing integration of observational and modeled outputs.88  
Similarly, the National Institute of Water and Atmospheric Research (NIWA) manages extensive environmental databases concerning atmosphere, freshwater, and marine domains.90 A prominent standard within NIWA's ecosystem is the River Environment Classification (REC), which organizes the physical characteristics of New Zealand's 425 million kilometers of river networks based on catchment climate, topography, and geology.91 These geospatial layers are made accessible via vector tile services and web map applications on the NIWA DataHub.92 The interoperability of New Zealand's spatial data is guided by the ANZLIC (Spatial Information Council) metadata profile, which adapts the international ISO 19115-1:2014 standard to local requirements, ensuring spatial metadata comprehensively describes geographic limitations, provenances, and usage constraints.80 Land Information New Zealand (LINZ) further supports this by managing vocabulary registries and encouraging the use of JSKOS (JSON for Knowledge Organization Systems) and SKOS for semantic artifacts.98

### **Australia: TERN and the Biodiversity Data Repository**

The Terrestrial Ecosystem Research Network (TERN) in Australia operates a highly advanced semantic infrastructure to harmonize ecological survey data across state, territory, and institutional jurisdictions.100 Overcoming the syntactic and semantic differences inherent in disjointed regional databases, TERN developed the TERN Ontology, a comprehensive information model describing field survey observations, sites, features of interest, samplings, and samples.100  
The TERN conceptual model relies heavily on SKOS-based controlled vocabularies stored in an RDF graph database, making them programmatically accessible via tools like SISSVoc (Spatial Information Services Stack Vocabulary Service).101 By representing elements such as categorical variables (e.g., plant growth forms) as SKOS collections, TERN guarantees that field data from divergent collection protocols can be unified at the conceptual core.102  
The TERN Ontology is formalised using SHACL to ensure data conformance and serves as the core ontology for the Australian Biodiversity Information Standard (ABIS).100 This standard facilitates the seamless transfer of data into the national Biodiversity Data Repository managed by the Australian Government.100 This robust infrastructure also enables powerful cross-organizational integrations, such as TERN's Memorandum of Understanding with Earthwatch Australia, which merges citizen-science monitoring of wetland and mangrove ecosystems with national-scale sensor networks using FAIR data principles.105 Additionally, TERN's data standards contribute to broader global initiatives like the Global Ecosystem Research Infrastructure (GERI).106

## **Synthesizing a Comprehensive Vocabulary-Backed Data Model**

To achieve true, global-scale interoperability, a modern environmental data model must synthesize the capabilities of the aforementioned technologies. It must integrate spatial topology, sensor observation logging, and formal ontological typing into a single, cohesive architecture.  
Such an implementation should utilize the **OGC Observations and Measurements (O\&M)** standard or the **SOSA/SSN** ontology as the conceptual core schema, acting as the skeletal structure linking sensors, samples, and geographic features.2  
For data storage and local programmatic implementation, **ODM2** provides the optimal relational database schema and Python API wrapper.10 The database tables for Action, Result, and SamplingFeature would be strictly constrained by ODM2's native structural vocabularies (e.g., ActionTypeCV, ResultTypeCV) to ensure transactional consistency.12  
To describe the thematic reality of the data, external controlled vocabularies must be referenced via stable HTTP URIs resolved by established semantic registries:

* The ObservableProperty field within the model should utilize the **CF Standard Names** (hosted via the NERC P07 SKOS collection) to guarantee precise, physically measurable definitions (e.g., specifying surface\_downward\_mass\_flux\_of\_carbon\_dioxide rather than an ambiguous "CO2 level").19  
* The FeatureOfInterest classification should be mapped directly to **ENVO** URIs (accessed via the EBI OLS or MMISW) to specify whether the sampling occurred in a tundra biome or an estuarine tidal riverine environment.17

Data serialization and exchange between institutional nodes would occur via **JSON-LD** payloads, incorporating the patterns established by the **ELFIE** and **SELFIE** initiatives over OGC API \- Features endpoints.75 This serialization guarantees that all relational attributes and taxonomic strings are machine-readable URIs. Finally, incoming data ingestion pipelines would employ **SHACL** shape constraints (such as those developed for KnowWhereGraph) to validate that the payloads adhere strictly to the expected ontology signatures (e.g., verifying that a CF parameter matches the expected ENVO feature class context), ensuring the long-term semantic integrity of the repository.8  
The successful deployment of this comprehensive architecture requires an ongoing commitment to the curation of these vocabularies, ensuring that as new environmental challenges arise, the semantic infrastructure remains agile, accurate, and globally interoperable.
